Bacteria Levels and Public Health Risk On the Malden River

Explore

Sampling Location

Each month, MyRWA volunteers collect water quality samples at monitoring stations throughout the Mystic River watershed as part of their Baseline Water Quality Monitoring Program.

This story focuses on bacteria data collected at one station (Station ID: MAR036) on the Malden River in the city of Medford, MA.

E. coli Samples

Each monthly water quality sample is analyzed for E. coli, which is a type of indicator bacteria.

Although E. coli bacteria are not necessarily harmful in and of themselves, they are commonly used as indicators of fecal contamination, which can contain other, more harmful bacteria species and viruses posing significant public health risk.

The chart to the right shows the E. coli level of each sample collected by MyRWA at their baseline monitoring station on the Malden River over the past 14 years.

But which samples indicate high public health risk? How frequently are the levels too high?

Boating Standard

Massachusetts state law includes a water quality standard for E. coli stating that no more than 10% of samples may exceed 1,260 #/100mL in waterways used primarily for boating.*

Samples above the standard (in red) indicate relatively high public health risk for people boating on the river due to the likely presence of fecal contamination.

Samples below the standard (in blue) indicate a low public health risk and the river would be relatively safe for boating.

Exceedence Frequency

By counting the number of samples above and below the standard, and then dividing by the total number of samples, we can estimate the percent of time that bacteria levels indicate high and low public health risk.

The bottom chart states that 30% of all samples over the past 14 years had E. coli levels exceeding the boating standard.

But why do some days have higher bacteria levels than others? Is there a way to predict whether E. coli levels are above or below the standard on any given day?

E. coli vs. Rainfall

Rainfall is a often good predictor of E. coli levels in urban rivers because it generates stormwater runoff that can contain high levels of bacteria.

The chart on the left shows the relationship between E. coli and total rainfall over the previous 48 hours of when each sample was collected.

The samples are categorized according to dry and wet weather. Samples collected after 0.25 inches or more of rainfall are considered as wet weather, the others as dry weather.

The chart on the right states that 23% of all samples were collected under wet weather conditions.

Do wet weather samples tend to have higher E. coli levels than the dry weather samples?

Wet Weather Exceedences

Based on the scatter plot, it looks like wet weather samples tend to exceed the standard more often than dry weather samples. Click this button to select only the wet weather samples.

We now see that 76% of wet weather samples were above the boating standard, and only 24% were below.

So on any given day, if it has rained 0.25 inches or more over the previous 48 hours, then we can expect a greater chance that bacteria levels will be too high.

Next, let's look at often dry weather samples exceed the standard.

Dry Weather Exceedences

Click this button to select only the dry weather samples.

Now we see that only 16% of the dry weather samples were above the standard, and 84% were below.

So even if there has been little or no recent rainfall, there is still a small chance that bacteria levels be too high and indicate a public health risk.

However, this chance is much lower than during wet weather, which was more frequently associated with high public health risk.

About

This interactive data visualization presents indicator bacteria data collected by the Mystic River Watershed Association (MyRWA) at their baseline monitoring program station on the Malden River from 2002 through 2015.

The Malden River is located within the Mystic River Watershed just north of the city of Boston, MA. A long history of industrial activity and urban development have resulted in extensive contamination of the sediments and water column, which pose significant public health risks due to elevated concentrations of various pollutants.

This visualization focuses on historical levels of E. coli, which is a type of fecal indicator bacteria commonly used to represent the public health risk associated with potential fecal contamination of freshwater lakes and rivers.

The goals of this visualization are to:

  1. Present historical bacteria levels measured by MyRWA at their monitoring station on the Malden River,
  2. Explain how bacteria levels relate to public health risk for boaters and other non-swimmers,
  3. Show how frequently measured bacteria exceed an acceptable limit, and
  4. Demonstrate how rainfall can be used to estimate whether bacteria levels will exceed that limit on any given day.
